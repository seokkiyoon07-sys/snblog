## 🏰 작품의 배경과 의미

「송인(送人)」은 고려 중기의 문인 정지상(鄭知常, ?~1135)이 지은 칠언절구(七言絕句)의 한시입니다. 대동강 남포 나루터에서 떠나는 사람을 배웅하며 지은 이별시로, 한국 한시 문학의 대표작 중 하나입니다.

정지상은 고려 중기의 뛰어난 시인이자 관료로, 묘청의 서경 천도 운동에 연루되어 김부식에 의해 죽임을 당했습니다. 그의 시는 섬세한 감수성과 뛰어난 서정성으로 유명합니다.

이 작품에서 화자는 비 온 뒤 풀빛이 짙어진 대동강변의 아름다운 풍경과, 그 속에서 이별해야 하는 슬픔을 대비시키고 있습니다. 자연의 생명력이 넘치는 배경이 오히려 이별의 슬픔을 더욱 부각시키는 효과를 만들어냅니다.

## English Description

## "Songin (送人)" - A Farewell That Never Ends

**Songin (送人, "Seeing Someone Off")** is a classical Chinese poem (hansi) written by Jeong Ji-sang (鄭知常, ?–1135), a poet and official of the Goryeo dynasty. Composed in the form of a seven-character quatrain (chileon jeolgu), it is regarded as one of the finest farewell poems in Korean literary history.

**Historical Background:**
Jeong Ji-sang was a distinguished poet and government official during the mid-Goryeo period. He was tragically executed in 1135 due to his involvement in Myocheong's campaign to relocate the capital to Seogyeong (Pyongyang). Despite his untimely death, his poetry survives as a testament to his exceptional lyrical sensitivity.

**Literary Significance:**

- **Seven-Character Quatrain**: Follows the classical qi-cheng-zhuan-jie (起承轉結) structure
- **Nature-Emotion Contrast**: Lush green scenery set against the sorrow of parting
- **Hyperbole**: Tears of farewell adding to the river's waters year after year
- **Rhetorical Question**: "When will the waters of the Daedong River ever run dry?"
- **Concrete Setting**: The Daedong River and Nampo ferry ground the poem in Korean geography

**Poetic Analysis:**
The poem achieves its emotional power through contrast—the vivid green of rain-washed banks against the grey of parting sorrow. The final couplet transforms personal grief into something cosmic: if farewell tears are added to the river year after year, the waters can never run dry, meaning sorrow itself becomes eternal and ever-renewing.

**Contemporary Relevance:**
The emotions in "Songin"—the sharpened beauty of the world when seen through the lens of loss, the understanding that grief renews itself with each passing year—remain profoundly relevant across time and cultures.
