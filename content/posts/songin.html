## "Songin (送人)" - A Farewell That Never Ends **Songin (送人, "Seeing Someone Off")** is a classical Chinese poem (hansi) written by Jeong Ji-sang (鄭知常, ?–1135), a poet and official of the Goryeo dynasty. Composed in the form of a seven-character quatrain (chileon jeolgu), it is regarded as one of the finest farewell poems in Korean literary history. **Historical Background:** Jeong Ji-sang was a distinguished poet and government official during the mid-Goryeo period. He was tragically executed in 1135 due to his involvement in Myocheong's campaign to relocate the capital to Seogyeong (Pyongyang). Despite his untimely death, his poetry survives as a testament to his exceptional lyrical sensitivity. **Literary Significance:** - **Seven-Character Quatrain**: Follows the classical qi-cheng-zhuan-jie (起承轉結) structure - **Nature-Emotion Contrast**: Lush green scenery set against the sorrow of parting - **Hyperbole**: Tears of farewell adding to the river's waters year after year - **Rhetorical Question**: "When will the waters of the Daedong River ever run dry?" - **Concrete Setting**: The Daedong River and Nampo ferry ground the poem in Korean geography **Poetic Analysis:** The poem achieves its emotional power through contrast—the vivid green of rain-washed banks against the grey of parting sorrow. The final couplet transforms personal grief into something cosmic: if farewell tears are added to the river year after year, the waters can never run dry, meaning sorrow itself becomes eternal and ever-renewing. **Contemporary Relevance:** The emotions in "Songin"—the sharpened beauty of the world when seen through the lens of loss, the understanding that grief renews itself with each passing year—remain profoundly relevant across time and cultures.
